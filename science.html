The Science

Focus vs Diffuse mode

Focus mode is when we're completely focused on a task, like taking an exam. Our brains perform just one thing. This activates the prefrontal cortex. Diffuse mode is the opposite. When we daydream or let our minds wander, many different areas of the brain are activated. Exercising and sleeping are forms of diffuse mode. We need both modes to learn effectively. We need to alternate between these two modes. Studying a little bit every day is better than craming everthing all at once. "Dual-task interference"

The Science of sleep

When we're awake, our brains produce toxic waste products. Sleep allows brain cells to shrink, facilitating fluid flow and toxin clearance. This energy-intensive process requires our body to shutdown. Sleep enables our brain to reorganize.

Brain training

Learning forms new neural connections. Repetition strengthens these connections. Our brains are constantly evolvingâ€”either growing or deteriorating. Applying focus and diffuse modes helps us build and maintain these neural connections.

Feedback

Negative feedback is more effective than positive feedback. By getting negative feedback, we know exactly what to fix. positive feedback is great for the begining, it makes us feel good. As we advance and ascend to higher levels of expertise in our field, negative feedback can be instrumental in our growth and development.

Procrastination

When learning something new, our brains often associate it with discomfort or fear of failure. This emotional response can lead to procrastination and seeking immediate gratification through distractions like social media. To overcome this, it's important to recognize that the initial discomfort of learning is a temporary challenge. While social media offers short-term pleasure, investing in learning and personal growth leads to long-term rewards and fulfillment. Happiness is crucial for avoiding procrastination.

Short and Long Term Memory

Short term memory AKA "working memory" our prefrontel part of our brain. Long term memory is all over our brain. We can usually hold 4 chunks of information when working with short term memory. Long term memory is created when the information in the short term memory is being used over and over. "Spaced Repetition"

Active VS Passive Learning

We learn more by active engagement. Listening to podcast or watching youtube videos or watching other people code is passive learning. Easy and not efficient. In order for connections to form, we need to take action. Passive learning feels good. It's easy and we feel good that we are learning. You can't learn how to play basketball by just watching games and never playing. Recalling the material is better than rereading. Before we reread a piece of information, it's best to try to remember as much as possible in order for the dots to connect. The illusion of competence is when we look at a solution of a problem without trying to solve it ourselves. One hour of test is better than one hour of study.

The science of motivation

There are two types of motivation: Intrinsic motivation: Comes from the inside. It fits our values and what we be belive in. And there's external motivation: money, salary and gifts. In order to learn we need intrinsic motivation. We need three things: 1. Autonomy- to have control. We want to work on things that gives us control over our lives. 2. Mastery- We are intrinsicly motivated by becoming a master in our field. 3. Purpose- What is my purpose? what is my destiny?

Goals

Setting big goals is exciting but also freitning. Our flight or flight mechanism is triggered when it senses large goals. We need to take small steps toward the goal. By doing that, we eliminate the fear of failure. Over long term, those small steps will compound. By forming small habbits and clear goals, we're more likely to accomplish our bigger goal long term. Well defiined specific goals is what we need.
SMART Goals:

  • Specific: Define the goal and make it clear and as specific as you can. For example: 1. Get healthy vs 2. Lose Weight vs 3. Lose 10 Pounds. The third goal is specific and will likely get better results than the previous two.
  • Measurable: We need to have a clear way to measure whether the goal was accomplished. If our goal was to climb the top of a mountain, it's a simple yes or no way of measurement. If our goal was to lose 10 punds we need a scale to determine if the goal was met.
  • Actionable: How will the goal be achieved? Do we have the resources to achieve it? If the actions required to achieve a goal are unclear or there are a large number of actions, we should consider breaking down the main goal into manageable, actionable sub-goals.
  • Relevant: A common issue we face is having too many goals at the same time, or pursuing the wrong goals. For this we can use the Pareto rule: Which are the 20% of goals that will provide me with 80% of my return?
  • Time bound: By including a specific date by which a goal should be accomplished, it helps provide incentive and allows us to monitor progress.
