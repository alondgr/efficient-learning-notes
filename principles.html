Learning VS Winning

In school we want to get good grades so that we pass the class, rather than actually take interest in what we're learning. Paul Graham's essay "The Lesson to Unlearn" argues that the traditional education system often prioritizes grades over true understanding. This focus on grades can lead to students prioritizing short-term memorization and test-taking strategies over developing a deep understanding of the subject matter. As a result, many students struggle to apply their knowledge in real-world situations. Graham suggests that to be successful, students need to unlearn this focus on grades and instead focus on developing a genuine curiosity and passion for learning. By doing so, students will be better equipped to solve problems, think critically, and innovate.

The Obstacle

As we learn and make progress we will encounter every now and then the feeling that we "suck". We can feel the distance from where we are right now and where we want to be. Sometimes it seems like we'll never get there. Change "you suck" to "good, I'm doing something worthwhile". If you succeed right away without making mistakes, that means you're not learning.

Compund Learning

Instead of learning in a marathon for 8 hours straight and then not learning for a week. Learn 20 minutes everyday. presistence is the key. we're in this for the long run.
"Improve by 1% a day, and in 70 days you're twice as good" -Alan Weiss, Ph.D.

Failues don't count

Nobody cares about your past failures. You can apply to 100 jobs and get rejected. On the 101st time, you can get accpeted, all these previous times won't matter. Your mistakes don't count against you. The more failures we encounter, the more feedback we will have to in order to improve.

Choice VS Chore and Reframing

If every time you study and learn, you approach it as a chore, long term, you're likely going to quit. Washing the dishes is a chore. Chores are temporary. Learning is a choice. We can reframe the concept of learning and look at it in a fresh new perspective. Instead of saying "I'm not good enough" say "I'm not there yet". Shift your perspective to what you can control instead of what you can't.

The Pareto Principle

The Pareto principle, also known as the 80/20 rule, states that a significant portion of results come from a small percentage of inputs. Roughly 80% of the results usually come from 20% of the causes. Eliminating something to its essence. What is the 20% that's going to get me 80% of the results?
